# Misdiagnosis — Notes

Conditions commonly confused with Pityriasis Versicolor (also known as Tinea Versicolor / Kleienpilzflechte). Not rendered on the website.

**Important:** Pityriasis Versicolor and Tinea Versicolor are the SAME condition — just different names. "Pityriasis" is the more modern/European term, "Tinea" is commonly used in the US. Both refer to the superficial fungal infection caused by Malassezia yeast.

## Conditions That Look Similar

### Vitiligo
- Autoimmune destruction of melanocytes → complete loss of pigment
- Patches are pure white (no pigment at all), PV patches are lighter but not pure white
- Vitiligo patches are usually symmetrical, PV is more random
- No flaking/scaling in vitiligo, PV has fine scaling
- KOH test negative in vitiligo

### Pityriasis Alba
- Common in children/young adults, especially atopic skin
- Pale, slightly scaly patches mostly on the face
- Less defined borders than PV
- Related to eczema/dry skin, not fungal
- Usually resolves on its own

### Pityriasis Rosea
- Starts with a single "herald patch" then spreads
- Oval-shaped patches following skin lines ("Christmas tree pattern" on back)
- Self-limiting — resolves in 6-8 weeks
- Possibly viral, not fungal
- Can look similar to PV rubra

### Tinea Corporis (Ringworm)
- Also fungal but different species (dermatophytes, not Malassezia)
- Classic ring-shaped lesions with raised, scaly borders and clearing center
- PV is flat patches without the ring pattern
- More itchy than PV typically
- Responds to different antifungals

### Seborrheic Dermatitis
- Also caused by Malassezia (same family of yeast!)
- Yellowish, greasy scales vs. fine dry scales in PV
- Concentrated on face, scalp, eyebrows, nasolabial folds
- More inflammatory and itchy than PV
- Can coexist with PV

### Contact Dermatitis
- Caused by irritants or allergens, not fungal
- Red, itchy, sometimes blistering
- Pattern follows exposure to irritant
- Resolves when trigger is removed

### Post-Inflammatory Hypopigmentation
- Lightened skin after inflammation/injury heals
- History of prior skin trauma, burn, or rash in that area
- No active scaling
- Not fungal — just melanocyte damage from prior event

### CARP — Confluent and Reticulated Papillomatosis
- Rare skin disorder, usually in adolescents and young adults
- Brown to hyperpigmented scaly macules or small papules
- **Key pattern:** Merge centrally and form a net-like (reticulated) pattern at the edges
- Most often on upper trunk and neck
- **Does NOT clear with antifungals** — this is a key differentiator from PV
- PV has positive fungal microscopy, CARP does not
- **Clinical rule of thumb:** Reticulated, slightly warty/papular brown lesions → think CARP; flat discolored patches with fine scale → think PV
- Texture matters: CARP is papular (slightly raised/bumpy), PV is flat

### Mycosis Fungoides (Cutaneous T-Cell Lymphoma)
- Very rare but important to rule out
- Early stages can look like PV (flat, discolored patches)
- Patches may be fixed in location and don't respond to antifungals
- Biopsy needed to diagnose
- If patches never clear with antifungal treatment, consider this

## Key Diagnostic Methods

### KOH Test (Potassium Hydroxide)
- Skin scraping + KOH dissolves skin cells, reveals fungal elements
- PV shows "spaghetti and meatballs" pattern (hyphae + spores)
- Quick and reliable when done properly

### Wood's Lamp (UV Light)
- PV fluoresces yellow-green under UV
- Vitiligo appears bright white
- Some tinea infections also fluoresce
- Not 100% reliable alone

### Skin Biopsy
- Gold standard for uncertain cases
- Rarely needed for PV
- Important if lesions don't respond to treatment (rule out lymphoma)

### Fungal Culture
- Can identify exact species
- Malassezia is difficult to culture (needs special lipid media)
- Not commonly done for PV

### Progressive Macular Hypomelanosis (PMH)
- **Nico specifically asked about this one!**
- White/light patches, typically on trunk (chest, back)
- Caused by a different organism: Cutibacterium acnes (Propionibacterium acnes)
- NOT fungal — bacterial
- Does NOT respond to antifungals (important!)
- No scaling (unlike PV which has fine scales)
- More common in young adults, darker skin tones
- Can coexist with PV, making diagnosis tricky
- Treatment: UV phototherapy, benzoyl peroxide, antibiotics
